Papa

Bernard Klepacki, Jr. 1938–2024

Some of my earliest memories are of my grandfather. I’m not even sure if they are real memories, or simply my imagination, spurred by his reminiscence, run wild.

But I remember him taking me to McDonald’s for a happy meal, and especially our trips after to the park to feed the ducks. I remember his little white truck. I know he babysat me and took care of me when I was brand new to this world.

I remember his big glasses and I remember so clearly the smell of his aftershave. His mischievous grin and the love in his smile.

I remember the elevator at Coronado Mall and the way he, for years, blamed my dad for nearly suffocating us all inside it. He was a dirty liar.

I remember him pulling quarters out of my ear and when I asked him how he did it he would simply say, “Magic,” and I knew there must be some trick to it and yet I kind of believed him because, to me, he was magic.

I remember him taking me and my sister to the pool when he was there for my graduation; when he was back for my sister’s graduation and we made him try on hats — not the caps he always wore but silly, cutesy, frilly ones — and we placed flowers in his hair. I remember his playfully scornful look and his boundless patience for us, his grandbabies.

And I realized, too, that he shaped my life. He was a lover of movies, maybe the biggest I knew. His shelves and shelves of DVDs captivated me. I remember going to the Suds & Cinema and the taste of the popcorn — still the best I’ve ever had — and the pizza — decidedly not the best I’ve ever had.

One of my favorite memories is when he took us to see Pirates of the Caribbean, and there was a float done up to look like a pirate ship in the parking lot, from which they were giving out gold coins (chocolate) and Mardi Gras beads — which for some reason made perfect sense for pirates when I was 10 years old. A film, I, heading into fifth grade, loved, based on a Disney World ride that I had ridden with him. Again, magic.

I believe his love of movies played a role in my journey into the very heart of the world of film, my own love of motion pictures. I am sure that for much of my professional life, he had no idea what it is I did. But I know that when he saw that my name was on-screen, in the credits of something he could watch from his own, favorite chair, he was proud.

I hope that he was proud. For all of his jokes about the powers of a Klepacki ID card, and for all of my own conflicted feelings about legacy and generational inheritance, I hope that he is proud. Proud that I bear his last name. Proud of the family he raised (imperfectly — yes), took care of, chose to be there for.

For so much of my life, Papa was the only extended family I had. When I was jealous of my friends with their big families and their reunions and all their cousins and aunts and uncles and grandparents . . . I didn’t have much, but I always had my Papa. He was always there. For me, he was there from the beginning. I have never known life without him, and it doesn’t seem right for him to be missing from it now.

I miss him. And I love him so much. I hope he knows how much he meant to me, his grandbaby.

I love you, Papa.

Published the 17th of September, 2024.
