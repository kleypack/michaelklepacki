For years I’ve read posts and memes, “ADHD be like…” “omg me and my ADHD brain,” and dismissively rolled my eyes — please, everyone experiences this; that’s just life; not everything is about your particular diagnosis. Only recently have I started to wonder, to question: is that everyone’s experience? Or is there a pattern I’ve been missing?

Some researchers are seeking to expand the diagnostic criteria for ADHD based on additional common categories of symptoms. On top of inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity — the three currently recognised by the DSM as screening tools — a recent study published by Cambridge University Press identifies an additional six, many of which hit home and give me a more structured way to understand my own experiences which I’ve seen mirrored in people diagnosed with ADHD.

Forgetfulness… could include difficulty keeping track of appointments, struggling to recall recent – or remote – events, retaining information, or remembering people's names during a conversation.

This is one I believe I’ve developed coping mechanisms to handle over the years: I live and die by my iCloud calendar for appointments; without auto-pay, I would’ve been buried long ago under late fees (of which I’ve paid many); I simply avoid new acquaintance’s names in conversation. I still walk out of my flat and make my way down the stairwell before immediately turning around about half the time to grab something I forgot.

People with ADHD often experience difficulties with activation – what many also call executive dysfunction, or ADHD paralysis. It’s difficulty starting and completing tasks, even important ones, or ones that feel interesting. The DSM describes avoidance occurring with unpleasant tasks, such as those that are mentally difficult, or chores.… Participants reported feeling stuck, like a state of inertia where the only thing that could motivate them to start or finish a task was a feeling of urgency, external pressures, or external help.

This is a huge one for me. Procrastination is perhaps the defining characteristic of my life. Every paper due for school was completed the night before — which usually meant I’m up all night. Every bill paid the very day it’s due (at best). Taxes filed on the 15th. Even little things, which then start to build up and only add to my anxiety and stasis. Texts from friends may go unanswered for weeks, and each friendly follow-up only stops me further in my tracks. Growing up all this led to massive burnout even as I performed quite well in school (until it became too much and I dropped out, twice); now, it leads to late fees and missed opportunities and guilt and shame and strained friendships.

People often mention difficulties with the intensity of their emotions… and many cite anger as the most difficult to manage, or the strong feelings associated with perceived rejection.

Many participants report that ADHD affects their perception of time.

If you know me, you know I am late to absolutely everything, all the time. I like to joke that time management is the one aspect of my life which proves I’m an optimist.

People may feel as if time slips away when they are not consciously paying attention to it, especially if they are engaged in a state of hyperfocus.

See: the beauty and curse of remote work. Also: the 16-hour days spent in Figma or Nova as my stomach eats itself and my spine calcifies.

Difficulties with time estimation can also impact how people with ADHD see tasks; they may overestimate how long a task that is deemed boring will take them to complete, reinforcing the avoidance associated with it, or underestimate how long a salient one takes – creating difficulties meeting deadlines, for example.

Many people with ADHD mention having difficulties sleeping. We know from previous studies that individuals with ADHD are more likely to have a delayed circadian rhythm (DSPS).

What, I’m a night owl!

I’m aware of the folly and dangers of self-diagnosis. But I also believe it’s unwise to ignore signs and avoid examination. I did well in school; took advanced classes, 4.2 GPA, 32 on the ACT. The kids I knew with ADHD got C’s and B’s and struggled with tests. We’re not the same, I told myself. Plus adults were always telling me how smart I was; I just needed to apply myself more, stop procrastinating, stop being so sensitive. I adopted their stigma and ignored my own struggles even as I crashed and burned.

Maybe I don’t have ADHD; perhaps I wouldn’t be diagnosed even with a rethinking of symptomatic criteria. But either way these are real problems in my life, things that cause me trouble and cost me money and generate a whole lot of anxiety. I think it’s time to stop brushing them off.
