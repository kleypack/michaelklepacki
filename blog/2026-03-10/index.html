The rain is about to come back and I don’t want it to though it is badly needed. The air has been hazy and brown-grey and dirty and sharp. The Seine is probably low and it’s been unseasonably, scarily warm and I love it even as it terrifies me. I am a reptile happy to sit upon a warm rock in the blinding noon until I am dizzy and drunk from the sun. The rain does not suit me, though once I longed for it, found an animalistic thrill in the wild august storms, the thunderous blue-hour downpours, the sky ripped open, fury and passion unleashed. But here this is no fury and there is no passion, almost never; no, there is just a steady monotonous unrelenting drizzle — if that — sometimes just a pervasive drifting mist. And once I dreamt of that, too, dreamt of any break from the dirt and the bright and the blue, dreamt of foggy green hills and cloud cover and cool air. But the green bears a cost. You must pay in warmth and light and joy, you must pay, pay dearly, pay until you are left soggy and sodden and downtrodden, day after day after day until you fear the light will blind you upon its return but you will gladly take the blindness for the warmth on your face. And anyway the green is not even here because here it is all stone and brick, all unnatural beige and white and grey, towers and walls of it, an endless labyrinth, and I am lost.
