Will I be relieved of the homesickness that’s been weighing on me? or about-face and long to be back here in France? or, most distressingly, find that I simply don’t feel at home eitherwhere?1 My present is a chaos of questions strewn about like toys from a sitcom closet and I am so deeply buried I cannot see but a couple days beyond; a suffocating, untenable way to live, I know. This is a crossroads I’m not sure I trust myself to navigate unaccompanied — not a small branch but a harsh parting, with the looks of a dead end but to the keenest eye; it is brush and bramble all round, but I cannot — I mustn’t — stop; onward the only way.

21 April 2026 at 22:59 CEST

For years I’ve read posts and memes, “ADHD be like…” “omg me and my ADHD brain,” and dismissively rolled my eyes — please, everyone experiences this; that’s just life; not everything is about your particular diagnosis. Only recently have I started to wonder, to question: is that everyone’s experience? Or is there a pattern I’ve been missing?

Some researchers are seeking to expand the diagnostic criteria for ADHD based on additional common categories of symptoms. On top of inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity — the three currently recognised by the DSM as screening tools — a recent study published by Cambridge University Press identifies an additional six, many of which hit home and give me a more structured way to understand my own experiences which I’ve seen mirrored in people diagnosed with ADHD.

Forgetfulness… could include difficulty keeping track of appointments, struggling to recall recent – or remote – events, retaining information, or remembering people's names during a conversation.

This is one I believe I’ve developed coping mechanisms to handle over the years: I live and die by my iCloud calendar for appointments; without auto-pay, I would’ve been buried long ago under late fees (of which I’ve paid many); I simply avoid new acquaintance’s names in conversation. I still walk out of my flat and make my way down the stairwell before immediately turning around about half the time to grab something I forgot.

People with ADHD often experience difficulties with activation – what many also call executive dysfunction, or ADHD paralysis. It’s difficulty starting and completing tasks, even important ones, or ones that feel interesting. The DSM describes avoidance occurring with unpleasant tasks, such as those that are mentally difficult, or chores.… Participants reported feeling stuck, like a state of inertia where the only thing that could motivate them to start or finish a task was a feeling of urgency, external pressures, or external help.

This is a huge one for me. Procrastination is perhaps the defining characteristic of my life. Every paper due for school was completed the night before — which usually meant I’m up all night. Every bill paid the very day it’s due (at best). Taxes filed on the 15th. Even little things, which then start to build up and only add to my anxiety and stasis. Texts from friends may go unanswered for weeks, and each friendly follow-up only stops me further in my tracks. Growing up all this led to massive burnout even as I performed quite well in school (until it became too much and I dropped out, twice); now, it leads to late fees and missed opportunities and guilt and shame and strained friendships.

People often mention difficulties with the intensity of their emotions… and many cite anger as the most difficult to manage, or the strong feelings associated with perceived rejection.

Check.

Many participants report that ADHD affects their perception of time.

If you know me, you know I am late to absolutely everything, all the time. I like to joke that time management is the one aspect of my life which proves I’m an optimist.

People may feel as if time slips away when they are not consciously paying attention to it, especially if they are engaged in a state of hyperfocus.

See: the beauty and curse of remote work. Also: the 16-hour days spent in Figma or Nova as my stomach eats itself and my spine calcifies.

Difficulties with time estimation can also impact how people with ADHD see tasks; they may overestimate how long a task that is deemed boring will take them to complete, reinforcing the avoidance associated with it, or underestimate how long a salient one takes – creating difficulties meeting deadlines, for example.

See above.

Many people with ADHD mention having difficulties sleeping. We know from previous studies that individuals with ADHD are more likely to have a delayed circadian rhythm (DSPS).

What, I’m a night owl!

I’m aware of the folly and dangers of self-diagnosis. But I also believe it’s unwise to ignore signs and avoid examination. I did well in school; took advanced classes, 4.2 GPA, 32 on the ACT. The kids I knew with ADHD got C’s and B’s and struggled with tests. We’re not the same, I told myself. Plus adults were always telling me how smart I was; I just needed to apply myself more, stop procrastinating, stop being so sensitive. I adopted their stigma and ignored my own struggles even as I crashed and burned.

Maybe I don’t have ADHD; perhaps I wouldn’t be diagnosed even with a rethinking of symptomatic criteria. But either way these are real problems in my life, things that cause me trouble and cost me money and generate a whole lot of anxiety. I think it’s time to stop brushing them off.

07 April 2026 at 22:58 CEST

Heavy. Hollow. Hard to describe just how I’m feeling. On the one hand: the images from Artemis II. Nearly brought to tears by the crescent Earth visible far beyond the looming lunar surface. All of us (well, all but four) somewhere on that little marble and none of us remotely perceptible. So small, indeed; and yet: the reason for it all, affirmed by those brave, crazy adventurers. The awe and dazzle evident in their voices so beautifully, painfully human. The distances we will travel, the unimaginable lengths we will push ourselves unto — only to be reminded that there is no place like home; our home and those we share it with is everything to us, all we have within the vast infinite. “We will always choose Earth. We will always choose each other.”

And all of that hope and conviction burns away on re-entry as I crash back down to the terrestrial surface. “A whole civilization will die tonight, never to be brought back again.” From up there, there is beauty and wonder; but down here, on the ground: just hatred and ego, petty and crude. “We are punching them while they’re down, which is exactly how it should be.”

Sick, actually. That about sums up how I feel. As I read these words and watch the jets and bombers take off as thousands line up hand-in-hand around their imperilled power plants and bridges; as inwardly I scream in desperation for revolt while I sit here and scroll in horror and… type. All the terror and shock and disgust that’s been mounting and multiplying for — how long has it been now? — and here we are, tipping perilously on the edge of fresh new unprecedented atrocity, yet another step far too far — how could we ever come back from this one? — and from that edge we continue to do absolutely nothing. We are all we have and yet we choose apathy, destruction, desecration, happy to pillage and rape and burn our miraculous little marble until it is barren and blackened as the dead grey moon.

Update:
Just minutes after posting this, a two-week ceasefire was announced. Which, thank god; but man, I am so fucking tired.

02 April 2026 at 00:26 CEST

The walls are sagging and warped and, possibly, rotting. The ceiling now sunk alarmingly low in the corner; oddly bowed upward near the door. The paint is bubbled and peeling, undoubtedly absorbing far too much moisture from the cold wet drafts that creep in around the rigid window frames. My foot keeps scuffing as I cross the room; the floor is too high here, no? And over there, why do the boards creak so? Always cold; again, the drafts, but — wait — is that a crack? Just here, arcing from floor to ceiling like a bolt of lightning, and where there is lightning there is thunder… yes; I hear it; rumbling, roaring, raging, I feel it. Can I patch this up? But the paint will peel anew and the cracks will grow and multiply until the walls are a hurricane because it is not the walls but lower, deeper. We built upon a bed of silt, or perhaps a bed of snakes. Indeed a miracle the walls have held this long. They may in fact hold for many years more, as the bubbles burst and blacken and the floorboards lift and splinter and the ceiling sags until you ponder if you should find something to prop it up as you work your way uphill to look out the window in wonder. As it all slowly crumbles, there, just outside: are the trees beginning to bloom?

Am I an opportunist for leaving? Am I a coward for staying away? I’d dreamt of leaving for so long, hadn’t I? And now I wonder if I ever should’ve left; equally filled with a desperate gnawing homesickness and dogged conviction to never return. But why shouldn’t I? In fact — shouldn’t I? Shouldn’t I fight? Not by standing in a plaza half the world away with tens of other disgruntled deserters, but by — what, exactly? What of those still manning the ship; those I left behind? In these desperate, maybe dying, days, shouldn’t we pull close, closer than ever before? Shouldn’t we have done that all along?

It is unnatural, to be so far from everything you know. Oh, what of nature in this world; but still. I don’t belong here; I am unmoored; adrift. Perhaps I simply never learned to belong. Perhaps to search, to flee, is all I know.

“It ebbs and flows” sounds too nice. “It comes in waves” too peaceful. Today it is here. The unbearable weight. The numb detachment. The vague darkness. Today it is a parasite, leeching and sucking and draining. Teased by tears that do not come, dried and hollowed out. Tired of fatigue, lethargic with blasé. Not a river, nor ocean, but perhaps a fog; imperceptibly rolling in, obscuring, chilling, isolating; then lifting as subtly as it came, driven by indiscernible changes in the dew point, whatever that is. How to raise the temperature, to alter the humidity? The things, the things to do, they do not always work; laughably imprecise tools. Yes, perhaps it is like the weather, elusive and ever-changing, driven by forces both too big and too small to suss out cause. I will not bother with a forecast. Tomorrow I will simply open the window.

13 March 2026 at 00:37 CET

The internet is written in ink, or so they say, but memory is a finger brushed across a foggy pane of glass. It has a fickle half-life. Breathe on the glass again, play a song or catch a scent, and perhaps you can still discern the lines and shapes. For how long?

How can there still be so much ahead when I can already hardly remember so little? I feel as if I am wandering aimless in the fog, no sense of where I am going and barely a trace of from where I have come. Faces, moments, entire lives that I once held so dear, that once wrecked me, that once meant the world to me, conversations and laughter and discoveries and obsessions that made me, shaped me — nearly lost, now formless abstractions within the void.

As a child I would lie awake well into the night and try to picture death, to wrap my head around eternity. Eternal nonexistence — it was the scariest thing I could imagine; so unknowable and permanent; it kept me up for hours. But these days it’s trying to remember that keeps me up, that fills me with baffled dread — because most of me is already dead, gone into that dark abyss. How have I lost so much? As if I woke up one day to find my apartment half-empty, the theft only more violent by the fact that I never noticed, couldn’t tell you what was taken when. It isn’t fair; I want it all back. But the moisture is evaporating back into thin air, and time, hardly any, has wiped the pane clean and those crude strokes have disappeared, never to be seen again.

10 March 2026 at 00:17 CET

The rain is about to come back and I don’t want it to though it is badly needed. The air has been hazy and brown-grey and dirty and sharp. The Seine is probably low and it’s been unseasonably, scarily warm and I love it even as it terrifies me. I am a reptile happy to sit upon a warm rock in the blinding noon until I am dizzy and drunk from the sun. The rain does not suit me, though once I longed for it, found an animalistic thrill in the wild august storms, the thunderous blue-hour downpours, the sky ripped open, fury and passion unleashed. But here this is no fury and there is no passion, almost never; no, there is just a steady monotonous unrelenting drizzle — if that — sometimes just a pervasive drifting mist. And once I dreamt of that, too, dreamt of any break from the dirt and the bright and the blue, dreamt of foggy green hills and cloud cover and cool air. But the green bears a cost. You must pay in warmth and light and joy, you must pay, pay dearly, pay until you are left soggy and sodden and downtrodden, day after day after day until you fear the light will blind you upon its return but you will gladly take the blindness for the warmth on your face. And anyway the green is not even here because here it is all stone and brick, all unnatural beige and white and grey, towers and walls of it, an endless labyrinth, and I am lost.

07 March 2026 at 02:36 CET

Lately I’ve been feeling restless. Wanting to stay up all night. It’s the depression, probably. I used to stay up for hours and hours into the night as a teenager. Usually on tumblr, or working on homework I had put off. These days I’m probably fiddling with this site (at best) or doomscrolling (not best, obviously).

It’s the depression, probably. But the reason I’m turning here, to this site surely no one but me sees, maybe, is because I’m watching a space I grew up loving be sucked of it’s soul. I used to love the web. It was somehow both a safe haven and an actual wild west within my sheltered desert home. It was where I raised Neopets until my mother needed the phone. Where I chatted on AIM with my improbable long-distance girlfriend. Where I discovered Homestar Runner and its ilk, leading to in-jokes with friends and my first ill-advised email address. Where I played games that required flash like slime basketball as my best friend sat next to me and we shared the keyboard. Where I sailed the Limewire seas to build up my music collection, because music was my best escape from the darkness but I was an adolescent with no allowance.1

But they are ruining the web. You know of whom I speak. They are stripping it of all humanity, all craft, all heart. Every day it is harder to find anything not devoid of humanity, anything you can trust, any place where you are not simply a collection of data points to be monetised. Content is king but content is dead; long live slop. They are destroying the place I love and I am angry.

And yet. I am heartened by those who still believe; who still handcraft and defend their own spaces and homes online; those who astound and inspire me with their knowledge of modern CSS and Javascript; those whose sites remind me of the AIM days; who write about music, the last films they watched, what’s inspiring them, what’s making them sad; who draw and code and read and share and type and strive and tinker and fiddle; those who fight; those who blog.2 I know I am not alone in my anger and my heart swells, if briefly, and I too want to fight.

And so… a blog. If you are here, thank you. If you’ve made it this far, hello, friend. I hope I can keep this up. Let’s fight together.
