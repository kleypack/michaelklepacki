MESH Admin Portal 2.0

To go along with the relaunch of MESH 2.0, administrators needed a functional new home to manage the platform. So I redesigned the Admin Portal from the ground up, along with a simple but comprehensive new design system.

When it was decided that MESH would relaunch in 2025 with a new, more stable foundation, the core user-facing app wasn’t the only thing that needed a big update. For years, administrators were using a backend platform built atop third-party libraries. Feedback was that it was slow and not at all intuitive, and I was assigned to fix the latter issue.

The team wanted something functional and lean. Make it clear and simple to use, built upon the same design principles I’d used for the MESH app — but it needed to be light and quick to implement, as the bulk of the dev team’s time needed to stay on the consumer app.

Design system #

As I started exploring designs for some of the portal’s main pages, I began to build out a design system. I created a straightforward colour palette accented by our brand dark blue with functional additions; specified a collection of necessary typographic styles; and curated a set of icons.

I then carefully selected the most important controls — like buttons, menus, dropdowns, and tags — that I knew would be needed across the platform for a component library.1 It was important not to sacrifice visual affordance, so subtle depth is used to cue interactivity.

The design system needed to be comprehensive but relatively simple to implement. I carefully selected the most important components, and pared them down without sacrificing visual affordance.

I also settled on a basic visual hierarchy and principles. At the top is a dark header with the site name and a logout option. The bulk of the page is filled by the flexible main content area, with a lower-contrast sidebar to the left providing persistent navigation, and a toolbar above — a very familiar desktop paradigm. Alternating row colours make table views easy to parse, and inset tabbed views should be immediately intuitive.

I pulled from classic desktop design for a structure that felt familiar and intuitive, using layout and contrast to help direct attention.

The new experience #

The dashboard is the first thing users see when they open the MESH app — a collection of pinned content that sits above the chronological news feed — so it’s the first thing administrators see upon logging into the admin portal. They can easily manage every item they’ve pinned here: grouping content with sections and reordering everything by dragging and dropping.

Administrators can manage dashboard sections and pinned content. Everything can be reordered by dragging.

Administrators can, of course, manage all content they create for MESH, such as posts, reels, and articles. Buttons are always at the ready for common commands like editing, deleting, and pinning to the dashboard. Articles — which are more time-consuming to create — can be saved as drafts before they’re published, so tags are used to communicate their status.

All content created for MESH can easily be viewed, edited, removed, or pinned to the dashboard.

Admins can also create certain content directly from the portal. Posts are simple, only allowing text, simple tags, and up to four images. Progressive disclosure is used when adding images, and accessibility is always ensured with mandatory alt-text fields.

Administrators can create new posts and include accessible images.

Articles include headlines, thumbnail images, and related reading in addition to all of the sub-items included in the main content itself. This required a more robust design solution than that for simple posts.

Articles are more complex to create and thus require a more complex design that should still be simple to understand.

Like all social networks, MESH needs moderators. When you allow almost anyone to create an account and post words and pictures, not everything will be appropriate. Moderators can use the admin portal to view reported content and profiles and take suitable action.

Moderators can easily view reported profiles and take appropriate action.

It was important to me to protect the well-being of our moderators as best we can. For reported posts containing images, the pictures are hidden until the moderator clicks “View,” and they can hit the Escape key on their keyboard to instantly hide them again.

Pressing the Escape key on the keyboard will instantly hide a reported post’s images, which may be shocking or disturbing.

Opportunities #

Opportunities are a super important part of MESH: mostly gigs or loans available for users to apply for, provided by companies MESH has partnered with. They’re key to making MESH more than a social network, giving users concrete ways to level up their careers; they also required a careful, multi-pronged solution to manage them behind the scenes.

Firstly, the partners themselves that provide the opportunities. Admins need to be able to add, edit, and delete partners, and ideally manage their respective opportunities. Simple enough.

Administrators can manage opportunity partners and get an overview of their respective opportunities.

Opportunities can also fall under a few categories — such as gigs and loans — and administrators need to be able to manage these, especially as opportunity offerings expand over time.

Opportunities can fall under different categories, and these categories may multiply over time.

And finally there are the opportunities themselves. A familiar table view lets administrators manage them with relevant commands.

Opportunities are listed for administrators to easily manage, with quick access to actions like downloading applications.

Like articles, there’s a lot to configure for each; I again used a tab view to break this up when creating or editing. The Details tab manages basic information, most of which is displayed on its card within the MESH app.

Opportunity details are displayed on their card within the MESH app to give users the most important information.

The description gives the user more info about the opportunity when they tap into the card. Administrators can add text and headings, lists, links, and images provided by the partner.

The description provides all information about an opportunity.

Finally, users must apply to opportunities to be considered. Partners can specify what personal information they need from applicants which administrators use to create a form.

Users must apply to be considered for opportunities; the Application tab makes it easy to create these forms.

Takeaways #

The admin portal was a massive undertaking, created simultaneously with the MESH 2.0 relaunch. It was my first time touching, or even seeing, the platform, and it was a great opportunity for me to focus on desktop design for a change.

We worked closely with the admin team to understand and assess their needs for the portal, delivering a much improved experience for managing all the content that makes MESH what it is. ☼
